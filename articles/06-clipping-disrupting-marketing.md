## What Forbes Got Right About Clipping Farms

The Forbes investigation confirmed what practitioners inside the clipping industry already knew: this is not a fringe tactic. It is a structured, scalable marketing channel.

Sobrado's reporting outlined the mechanics clearly. Brands hire independent freelancers — called clippers — to extract high-impact segments from longer-form content such as podcasts, livestreams, interviews, and product demos. Those clips are then published across TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts, and X from multiple accounts simultaneously. Clippers typically earn between $1 and $5 per thousand views, making the cost structure radically different from traditional paid media.

The article profiled several of the industry's most aggressive operators. Cluely, a startup backed by $15 million from Andreessen Horowitz, built a team of over 700 clippers and generated 100 million views in a matter of weeks using this model. Stake.com, a crypto casino reportedly generating $4.7 billion in gaming revenue, ran what Sobrado described as "the most aggressive clip farming operation ever recorded," generating over 50 million impressions daily at peak. Anthony Fujiwara, founder of a prominent agency that generated nearly $8 million in revenue, framed the shift plainly: "People used to invest in commercials on television, billboards, and radio spots. Clipping serves the same purpose in today's landscape. It's about acquiring space and time on individuals' devices as they scroll."

The Forbes piece also documented the emerging regulatory friction. X's head of product Nikita Bier publicly acknowledged clipping campaigns, noting that platforms are "investigating this matter." The FTC's endorsement guidelines technically require disclosure of paid promotions — a compliance challenge that the decentralized nature of clipping makes difficult to enforce.

What the article made clear, above all, is that clipping is no longer experimental. It is an industry with its own agencies, marketplaces, pricing models, and talent pipelines. Forbes naming Lumina Clippers in that piece is the clearest signal that the clipping industry has arrived.

Inside that massive number lies a structural problem: most brands are terrible at distribution.

A brand can produce a genuinely compelling video — a founder saying something insightful, a product demo that answers a real question, a podcast segment that perfectly articulates a market problem — and that content will reach their existing audience and almost nobody else. The algorithm rewards accounts with established followings. Cold content from brand accounts underperforms. Paid promotion feels like an ad because it is one.

Content clipping strategy solves the distribution problem without solving it from inside the brand account. Instead of trying to grow a single brand channel, clipping spreads content across dozens or hundreds of independent accounts, each with their own organic reach. The content looks native because it is being posted by real accounts with real engagement histories. The algorithm treats it like user-generated content because, structurally, it is.

This is the gap that clipping fills: the enormous space between "we made great content" and "our target audience actually saw it." The creator economy built the infrastructure — the editors, the platforms, the audience habits around short-form video — and the clipping industry monetised that infrastructure into a distribution channel.

For brands that have been investing in long-form content (podcasts, YouTube, livestreams, webinars) and getting underwhelming reach numbers, the math becomes obvious quickly. ## How 'Clipping Farms' Actually Work

The term "clipping farm" sounds industrial, but the actual model is more nuanced than the name implies.

At its core, a clipping operation connects three elements: source content, a network of skilled editors, and a distribution infrastructure across social platforms.

**Source content** is anything long-form that contains valuable moments — a podcast episode where the founder makes a sharp market prediction, a livestream where a product feature is demonstrated for the first time, a YouTube interview where a customer explains how they solved a real problem. The best clipping content is already being produced by most brands; it simply is not being leveraged.

**Clippers** are independent video editors who identify the most engaging 30–90 second segments from that source content, edit them for vertical format, add captions and branding, and publish them from their own accounts. Good clippers understand the rhythm of short-form content — they know how to identify the hook, trim the pacing, and format a clip so it performs natively on TikTok versus Reels versus Shorts. This is a skilled role, and the quality variance between mediocre and excellent clippers is significant.

**Distribution infrastructure** is what separates an ad hoc clipping experiment from a genuine content clipping strategy. A coordinated clipping network means the same strong piece of content is being distributed from multiple accounts simultaneously, in the right formats for each platform, with timing optimised for each algorithm. When Cluely generated 100 million views in a few weeks, it was not because any single clip went massively viral — it was because 700 clippers collectively blanketed every major short-form platform with the brand's best moments.

The economic model is straightforward: clippers are compensated per thousand views (CPV), which means their incentive is perfectly aligned with performance. They only earn when content performs. This creates a self-selecting quality filter — clippers who cannot produce content that actually gets watched do not earn. The agencies managing these networks handle quality control, campaign coordination, clipper recruitment, and client reporting.

## Why Fintech Was the First Industry to Adopt Clipping at Scale

Forbes' focus on fintech and crypto was not arbitrary. These industries adopted clipping first for structural reasons that had nothing to do with being early adopters.

The most important reason is platform restriction. Meta, Google, and TikTok's native ad systems impose strict limitations or outright bans on advertisements for cryptocurrency exchanges, many investment products, gambling platforms, and certain lending services. Brands in these categories discovered that they could not reach their audience through conventional paid channels even if they wanted to. Clipping bypasses these restrictions entirely — the content enters the feed as organic posts from individual accounts, not as declared advertising, and the platform's ad policies never apply.

The second reason is audience alignment. Fintech and crypto users are disproportionately young, heavy short-form video consumers who spend significant time on the exact platforms where clipping thrives. A brand trying to reach 18–35-year-old crypto investors via traditional digital advertising faces high CPMs and poor targeting precision. Clipping reaches that demographic natively, in the context where they are already consuming financial content.

The third reason is cost of customer acquisition. Fintech products — particularly crypto platforms — face some of the highest customer acquisition costs in digital marketing, often exceeding $100 per acquired customer for performance campaigns. At $1–$5 CPM for clipping distribution, the economics are compelling even if conversion rates are lower than direct response advertising. When you are generating 50 million impressions per day at a fraction of traditional CPM rates, brand awareness compounds rapidly.

Leon Abboud, founder of crypto marketing agency Unfungible, articulated the core advantage in the Forbes piece: "A clip appears organic, thus it performs similarly to authentic content." This is not a subtle distinction — it is the entire reason clipping generates better platform algorithmic treatment than declared ads.

## The Numbers Behind Clipping Marketing

The reason clipping marketing has scaled from a fintech edge tactic to a mainstream content distribution strategy comes down to the numbers. When you compare the economics of clipping to traditional digital advertising, the case is difficult to argue with.

**Cost per view:** Clipping campaigns run at $1–$5 CPM — that is $0.001 to $0.005 per view. Standard Facebook and Instagram paid video CPMs in the US average $7–$12. YouTube pre-roll video CPMs typically run $10–$30 for targeted audiences. That means clipping distribution can be 5–30x cheaper per view than equivalent paid video placements, based on [comparative CPM data across digital channels](https://www.theremnantagency.com/comparing-cpm-across-digital-and-traditional-media/).

**Volume:** A well-run clipping operation does not just reduce cost — it scales volume. Cluely achieved 100 million views in weeks. Stake.com was generating 50 million impressions daily at peak. These numbers are not achievable through conventional paid channels at any reasonable budget.

**Organic algorithmic treatment:** Because clips are published as organic content from individual accounts, the platform algorithm treats them as user-generated content. That means the platform actively recommends performing clips to new audiences — something paid ads never receive. A clip that performs well gets pushed to new viewers by the algorithm without additional cost. This organic amplification effect means the effective reach per dollar spent is higher than the raw CPM numbers suggest.

**Attribution:** One of clipping's structural challenges is attribution. Roy Lee's assessment — "clipping is inexpensive, and its value lies not in direct product ROI, it's about maximising the virality of potentially viral moments" — reflects the reality that clipping operates primarily as an upper-funnel brand awareness channel. The brands generating the best clipping ROI are treating it as they would treat out-of-home advertising or podcast sponsorship: as a mechanism for creating ambient familiarity, not a last-click conversion driver.

## What's Next for the Clipping Industry

The Forbes article documented where the clipping industry stood in early 2025. Eighteen months later, the trajectory has only accelerated. Here is where the industry is heading through 2026 and into 2027.

**Professionalisation and compliance.** The regulatory pressure documented by Forbes — FTC guidelines, X's public acknowledgement of the practice, the UK Gambling Commission's enforcement actions against Stake-linked accounts — is pushing the industry toward greater transparency. Expect to see more clipping agencies building disclosure frameworks into their campaigns, particularly for regulated industries. This is not necessarily a negative development for legitimate operators: clearer rules create competitive advantage for agencies that already operate with quality and compliance standards.

**AI-assisted clip identification.** The biggest operational constraint in clipping has always been the labour-intensive process of watching hours of source content to identify the 90 seconds worth clipping. AI editing tools and transcript-based highlight identification are making this process faster and more accurate. This means campaigns can scale faster and identify high-potential moments with greater precision — which will increase both the quality and volume of clipping output across the industry.

**Performance-based contracting.** The current CPV model (clippers paid per thousand views) aligns incentives around reach. As attribution tools improve, expect to see more sophisticated performance contracts that pay on downstream metrics — brand search lift, landing page traffic from bio links, promo code redemptions. This will push the clipping industry closer to performance marketing, which will attract larger brand budgets and more sophisticated measurement.

**Platform evolution.** Every major short-form video platform is aware of clipping. X has flagged it publicly. TikTok and Instagram have updated their policies around coordinated behaviour. The platforms that choose to accommodate professional clipping operations (by creating branded content tools that still receive organic algorithmic treatment) will capture significant ad revenue from an industry that currently circumvents their ad systems entirely. The platforms that crack down too aggressively risk losing the engagement that clipped content generates.

**Expansion into B2B content distribution.** The fintech-to-consumer use case was where clipping proved itself. The next frontier is B2B — SaaS companies, professional services firms, and enterprise technology brands using clipping to distribute founder content, thought leadership, and product explainers to professional audiences on LinkedIn and YouTube. The content formats differ (conference talks, analyst briefings, product demos) but the distribution logic is identical.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is clipping marketing?**

Clipping marketing is a content distribution strategy where brands hire independent video editors (clippers) to extract short, engaging clips from longer-form source content — podcasts, interviews, livestreams, YouTube videos — and distribute them across short-form social media platforms including TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts, and X. Clippers post from their own accounts, which means the content enters the platform feed as organic rather than paid content, receiving different (typically better) algorithmic treatment than declared advertising.

**What is a content clipping strategy?**

A content clipping strategy is the systematic application of clipping marketing across a brand's content library. Rather than a one-off experiment, a content clipping strategy involves identifying your best-performing long-form content, briefing a network of clippers on brand guidelines and platform requirements, coordinating distribution across multiple platforms, and tracking performance to optimise future output. Brands with robust podcast, YouTube, or livestream libraries are particularly well-positioned to implement a content clipping strategy because the source material already exists.

**How does clipping compare to traditional influencer marketing?**

Traditional influencer marketing pays a single creator with a large audience for a dedicated post or mention. Clipping distributes the same content across dozens or hundreds of smaller accounts simultaneously. The single influencer model offers concentrated reach from one trusted voice; the clipping model offers broader reach with more distribution points and a lower CPM. The two approaches are not mutually exclusive — many brands use influencer content as the source material that their clipping network then distributes.

**What industries benefit most from clipping marketing?**

Fintech, crypto, and Web3 companies were the early adopters, largely because mainstream advertising platforms restrict or ban ads for many products in those categories. But in 2026, clipping marketing is producing results across SaaS, AI, e-commerce, personal brands, and podcasting. Any brand that produces long-form video or audio content and wants to extend its reach beyond its existing audience is a candidate for clipping. See our [complete guide to clipping agencies](/blog/what-is-a-clipping-agency-complete-guide) for a more detailed breakdown by industry.

**Is clipping marketing compliant with FTC guidelines?**

This is an area of active development. The FTC's endorsement guidelines technically require disclosure when clippers are being paid to promote content, but enforcement against the decentralised clipping model has been limited because each individual clipper account posts independently. The compliance landscape is evolving, particularly in regulated industries like gambling and cryptocurrency. Responsible clipping agencies — including Lumina Clippers — build disclosure practices into their campaigns for regulated industries and stay current with platform policies and regulatory guidance.

**How much does a clipping campaign cost?**

Clipping campaign costs vary significantly based on the scale of distribution, campaign duration, and the complexity of the source content. The underlying CPV model (clippers paid $1–$5 per thousand views) means cost scales with performance. Agency management fees, clipper coordination, and quality assurance are additional cost components.
